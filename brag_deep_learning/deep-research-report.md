# Deep Research on Whether Deep Learning and Transformers Belong at the Frontier Beyond OpenGATE for Bragg Peak Simulation

## Core conclusion

Your instinct is basically right: a project whose goal is only “a faster surrogate that is almost as accurate as OpenGATE” is **not** the deepest frontier anymore. OpenGATE is not a hand-tuned shortcut model; it is an open-source Monte Carlo particle transport platform built on Geant4, and GATE 10 has recently been rebuilt around a Python interface, multithreading and multiprocessing, library embedding, and direct in-memory coupling to other software. In other words, the baseline is already a serious first-principles simulator, not a simplistic manual approximation. citeturn27view0turn15view3turn25search0

If your goal is truly frontier-grade, there are only two directions that look genuinely important. The first is **physics-preserving acceleration**: make a system that is dosimetrically indistinguishable from Monte Carlo for the intended use case, but faster, ideally by improving transport, parallelization, variance reduction, or tightly constrained learned components. The second is **reality-preserving improvement**: beat current planning chains not merely against OpenGATE or Geant4, but against measurements, imaging-derived stopping-power truth, LET-sensitive endpoints, and ultimately patient outcomes. Recent reviews and method papers point to exactly these two pressure points: computation time on one side, and input/biology uncertainty on the other. citeturn15view14turn32view3turn32view0

In plain English, there are two different races here. One race is “make the simulator faster without changing the answer that matters.” The other race is “make the answer closer to what actually happens in patients.” A lot of AI papers only compete in the first race. The harder and more meaningful frontier is to win **both**. citeturn15view14turn28view4turn32view3

## What improving OpenGATE really means

The most important correction to the framing is this: **improving OpenGATE does not necessarily mean replacing it with a black-box network**. GATE 10’s new architecture was explicitly designed to be embedded in larger software stacks, to exchange data directly from memory, and even to support alternative task-specific back ends. The GATE 10 architecture paper goes further and explicitly discusses future fast-tracking back ends specialized for proton dose calculation, as well as callback-triggered variance-reduction ideas in which an AI-based function could generate particle exits from a volume instead of tracking every step through it. That is a hybrid frontier, not a pure-replacement frontier. citeturn27view5turn27view3

That matters because the best non-AI work in this space is still very strong. A recent stochastic differential equation proton dose model reproduced Geant4 with range differences within 0.4 mm and 3D gamma pass rates above 98% under 3%/2 mm criteria, while running about five times faster. A separate 2026 GPU Fast Dose Calculator beam-modeling study validated against **2,744 IMPT QA measurements** across **269 plans** and **155 patients**, with overall mean gamma passing rates of **98.5%** for the independent MC engine versus **98.7%** for RayStation under matched conditions. Those are exactly the kinds of results that define a real challenger to OpenGATE-style truth engines: measurement-backed, clinically legible, and not merely “looks good on a held-out dataset.” citeturn22academia20turn31view0turn31view2

There is also a second frontier that is easy to miss if one focuses only on transport speed: **the simulator can only be as realistic as its inputs**. A recent virtual photon-counting CT study showed that CT-to-stopping-power conversion remains a major bottleneck for proton accuracy, with Bragg-peak misplacement at the distal edge tracing directly to SPR mismatch; in that study, a spectral PCCT approach achieved an overall SPR error of **0.28%** and more closely reproduced ground-truth dose distributions than single-energy approaches. That means “more accurate than OpenGATE” may come less from a new beam-transport network and more from a better imaging-to-material pipeline feeding the simulator. citeturn32view0

In plain English, if OpenGATE is the physics engine of a race car simulator, then accuracy depends on more than the engine. It also depends on whether you mapped the track correctly and whether your tire model matches the real road. In proton therapy, those extra pieces are things like stopping-power conversion, anatomy change, beam model commissioning, LET, and biological response. citeturn32view0turn32view3turn8search1

## What deep learning and transformers have already achieved

Yes, this has already been done—substantially. In 2021–2022, Pastor-Serrano and Perkó introduced a transformer-based view of proton particle transport and then the **DoTA** dose engine. The transformer paper framed dose calculation as sequence modeling over beam’s-eye-view slices and reported a **99.59% gamma pass rate at 1%/3 mm**, greatly outperforming earlier data-driven proton methods. The later DoTA paper reported training on **80,000** proton beamlet geometries, **5 ms** beamlet predictions, and **99.70% plan-level gamma at 2%/2 mm** across test patients, with full treatment-plan doses in about **10–15 s**. citeturn14search0turn19academia17

The field did not stop there. In 2025, Mayo Clinic published **“Proton dose calculation with transformer”**, but despite the title the model was a **SwinUNetr** architecture operating on CT plus a projected proton spot map. It was trained on **259 prostate SBRT plans** and fine-tuned on **84 CNS plans**, reaching **0.07 s** single-field inference on an Nvidia A100 and gamma indices around **92.2%** for prostate dose-to-water and **90.1%** after CNS transfer learning under 2% local dose difference / 2 mm criteria. That is clinically interesting and fast, but it is not yet the same “near-Monte-Carlo parity” story as DoTA or ADoTA. citeturn18view1turn18view2

In 2025, the Heidelberg group introduced **CC-LSTM** for mono-energetic proton pencil-beam dose estimation in adaptive proton therapy. Trained on **90 low-grade glioma patients**, the model raised the average local gamma-index pass rate at **1%/2 mm** from **92.54%** to **97.21%** over an earlier RNN baseline, while also reducing parameters by **98.8%** relative to the then-state-of-the-art comparator. That paper is especially important because it explicitly targeted **unseen delivered treatment plans** and adaptive use, not just idealized offline prediction. citeturn21view0

In 2026, **ADoTA** pushed the transformer line further by solving one of the awkward practical issues in earlier proton dose engines: beam-angle handling. ADoTA encoded beam direction analytically instead of rotating the CT grid, trained on **108 patients** and tested on **50 independent patients**, reported beamlet gamma pass rates of **99.40%** in thorax and **99.87%** in abdomen/pelvis at **1%/3 mm**, **1.72 ms** single-beamlet inference, an **~86%** reduction in end-to-end 3D dose computation relative to the fastest reinterpolation-based methods, and plan-level gamma of **98.4%** for lung and **98.9%** for prostate at **2%/2 mm**. That is one of the clearest examples that transformer-like architectures are not a hypothetical idea here; they are already part of the state of the art. citeturn17view0turn17view1turn17view2turn17view3turn17view4turn17view5

Deep learning has also moved beyond “predict dose from CT.” In 2025, a direct **MRI-only proton dose** study for brain tumors reported Monte-Carlo-comparable accuracy with about **0.2% median dose error** and millisecond-level beam dose prediction, aiming to bypass synthetic CT entirely. A 2025 **CBCT direct-dose** study used **xLSTM** networks for adaptive proton therapy on paired planning CT and treatment CBCT. In 2026, Fourier Neural Operators were proposed for fast prediction of proton transport and neutron production for real-time range verification, showing that learned operators are expanding from dose prediction into transport observables relevant for beam monitoring. citeturn22search0turn30search14turn23search3

There is also a more conservative AI line that may be closer to clinical reality in the near term: using learning to **support** Monte Carlo rather than replace it. A 2022 plan-verification platform used deep-learning-based Monte Carlo denoising to preserve MC accuracy while cutting runtime for online adaptive proton therapy. This kind of design is often more attractive clinically because the core object remains “Monte Carlo dose,” but AI removes noise or cost. citeturn33search0turn33search4

In plain English, the answer to “has this been done?” is a clear yes. Transformers, sequence models, MRI-only dose engines, CBCT dose engines, LET predictors, and MC denoisers have all been explored. The question is no longer whether AI can learn the Bragg-peak-related transport pattern. The real question is which pieces of the workflow should be learned, how tightly physics should constrain them, and what kind of validation makes them trustworthy. citeturn14search0turn19academia17turn21view0turn17view0

## How caught up the field is to modern deep learning

The field is **partly** caught up to modern deep learning, but not fully. On the one hand, proton-therapy ML has clearly moved beyond plain U-Nets: the literature now includes transformers, SwinUNetrs, ConvLSTM/xLSTM variants, multimodal pipelines, operator-learning ideas, and growing interest in uncertainty estimation and hybrid physics-informed models. Recent reviews explicitly describe LLMs and multimodal AI as emerging contributors to proton-therapy research and decision support, and GATE 10 itself was designed so that external AI tools can be coupled directly into simulation workflows. citeturn28view1turn28view3turn27view3

On the other hand, this is still **not** a foundation-model field in the sense people usually mean in frontier AI. Most core dose papers are narrow supervised models trained at a single institution, on tens of patients to a few hundred plans, for a tightly defined task. Representative examples include **90 patients** for CC-LSTM, **108 training / 50 test patients** for ADoTA, **259 prostate plus 84 CNS plans** for the Mayo transformer-dose study, and **39 brain tumor patients** for MRI-only direct proton dose. Even when the number of beamlets is large, the number of unique patients and acquisition settings is still comparatively small. The 2025 and 2026 reviews repeatedly flag **data scarcity**, **generalizability**, **uncertainty**, and the scarcity of **prospective validation** as the bottlenecks to routine clinical translation. citeturn21view0turn17view2turn18view2turn22search7turn28view0turn28view1turn28view3turn28view4

A sign that the field is maturing is the appearance of better public benchmarks. The **DoseRAD2026** challenge released paired CT/MRI data for **115 patients** with **81,000 proton beamlets** and open-source Monte Carlo ground truth for benchmarking fast proton dose methods in adaptive settings. That is a meaningful step toward reproducibility. But by frontier-AI standards, it is still small, and it is brand new. My own inference from the papers above is that proton-therapy deep learning is currently in a stage similar to where medical imaging was before large benchmark ecosystems became dominant: technically impressive, clinically promising, but still dataset-constrained and validation-constrained. citeturn7academia23turn11search5turn28view0

In plain English, the field has modern **architectures**, but not yet modern **scale**. It knows about transformers; it does not yet have the equivalent of a huge multi-center “foundation dose model” that everybody trusts. That gap is one reason a pure black-box replacement of OpenGATE is still a hard sell. citeturn17view0turn28view0turn28view3

## Would a learned model actually be trusted and used in proton therapy

Potentially yes—but almost certainly **not first as an unconstrained black-box replacement for OpenGATE**. The clinical and regulatory pattern in radiotherapy is to trust AI first in **adjacent workflow roles** that are easier to bound, review, and override. The FDA’s public AI-enabled-device list explicitly exists to identify marketed AI devices, but it also states that the list is **not comprehensive** and that the agency is still exploring how to identify devices that use foundation models and other modern AI systems. At the device-software level, FDA guidance now operates under the Quality Management System Regulation harmonized with ISO 13485, which reinforces lifecycle validation and quality controls rather than “it worked once on a test set.” citeturn15view10turn15view11

You can already see the trust pathway in actual radiotherapy devices. **MRI Planner** uses pretrained ML for synthetic CT generation and contouring, but its outputs are intended for trained oncologists, dosimetrists, and physicists and are evaluated by dosimetric agreement and gamma-style testing. **AI-Rad Companion Organs RT** automatically contours CT data with deep learning, but the FDA-cleared labeling explicitly says the results must be reviewed, edited, and accepted by trained professionals and that the underlying models are locked. Meanwhile, standard commercial TPS products like **Monaco** are cleared to calculate proton dose, but that is not the same thing as an FDA-cleared AI primary proton dose engine. In the public sources I found for this search, AI is already trusted around the radiotherapy workflow; a clearly marketed AI system acting as the primary proton dose calculator was not similarly visible. Because the FDA list itself is not exhaustive, that last point should be read as a cautious search finding, not a proof of nonexistence. citeturn37view0turn37view1turn37view2turn15view10

The professional guidance points the same way. Broad radiotherapy AI implementation literature has long emphasized commissioning, quality assurance, and monitoring as prerequisites for clinical use, and more recent consensus guidance for radiotherapy AI deployment highlights model selection, clinical commissioning, day-to-day QA, and ongoing review. The 2025 proton-therapy AI review likewise says few approaches have undergone prospective validation and that regulatory pathways are still evolving; it specifically calls for reproducibility, clinical reliability, and safe deployment through standardized benchmarks and multi-institutional collaborations. citeturn12search1turn12search3turn28view3

For proton therapy specifically, trust also depends on what you mean by “more accurate.” A learned model that matches OpenGATE’s dose map better is not automatically more clinically truthful, because the true bottlenecks may be elsewhere: CT-to-SPR conversion, range uncertainty, LET/RBE biology, or anatomy change. The LET review is especially important here. It notes that fixed **RBE = 1.1** is an oversimplification, that high LET in organs-at-risk has been associated with adverse events, that commercial TPSs now have fast MC for routine dose calculation, but that fast-MC LET is not yet in commercial TPSs, and that proton centers increasingly want LET/RBE calculation and visualization tools. So a clinically trusted “better-than-OpenGATE” system may need to be better not only at physical dose but also at biological relevance. citeturn32view3

My judgment is that a learned proton model **would** be used if it cleared a much higher bar than most current papers meet. It would probably enter the clinic first as one of three things: a secondary checker against a conventional plan, a speedup module inside a physics engine, or a planning-support component for adaptive workflows. Only after locked-model validation, external measurement agreement, prospective testing, uncertainty estimation, and multi-center robustness would a center consider depending on it as the primary source of truth. That conclusion is an inference from the current validation culture, regulatory pattern, and AI implementation guidance—not a formal prohibition against learned dose engines. citeturn31view2turn15view10turn12search3turn28view3

In plain English, clinics do not trust a model because it is clever. They trust it because it is **audited, measured, bounded, monitored, and overridable**. If a transformer eventually becomes the most accurate thing available, it can absolutely become clinically important—but it will probably earn trust by acting like a very well-behaved part of a larger safety system, not by arriving as a mysterious black box that asks everyone to stop believing Monte Carlo. citeturn12search1turn12search3turn15view11

## Where the real frontier is if your goal is better than OpenGATE

If the project goal is “push the frontier beyond OpenGATE,” the strongest research program is a **hybrid physics-plus-learning stack** rather than a pure learned emulator. GATE 10 now creates the software conditions for exactly that kind of work: scriptable Python control, in-memory data exchange, coupling to external AI tools, and a path toward task-specific transport back ends. A serious frontier project would use GATE 10 or a Geant4-derived truth engine as the reference core, then add learned components only where they solve an identifiable bottleneck. citeturn27view0turn27view3turn27view5

The first bottleneck worth attacking is **speed without physics drift**. That suggests learned variance-reduction modules, Monte Carlo denoising, beam-angle-aware learned beamlet engines like ADoTA, or transport surrogates that stay bounded against MC and measurements. The 2024 fast-MC review explicitly notes that AI can greatly speed dose calculations but currently pays a price in generalizability, and it contrasts that with hardware-centric acceleration paths that can preserve broader validity. That is why the smartest frontier design is likely not “replace OpenGATE with a transformer,” but “wrap learned accelerators around a truth engine and prove where they are allowed to operate.” citeturn15view14turn17view0turn33search0

The second bottleneck is **input realism**. If your aim is better agreement with real datasets, then CT-to-RSP, PCCT/DECT, CBCT correction or direct CBCT dose, MRI-only planning, and automated beam commissioning are all part of the frontier. Recent work shows that proton range and Bragg-peak placement still suffer when stopping-power estimation is wrong, that spectral PCCT can reduce SPR error and improve dose fidelity, and that automated multi-engine MC commissioning can standardize beam modeling using clinical cases and measurements. This is one of the clearest ways to become “more accurate than current OpenGATE-based practice” without pretending the transport kernel itself was the entire error budget. citeturn32view0turn33search6

The third bottleneck is **biological realism**. If the simulator stops at physical dose, it is still leaving frontier clinical signal on the table. Reviews on LET in proton therapy show why: adverse events correlate with high LET in organs at risk, and centers increasingly want LET/RBE-aware planning and evaluation. There are already deep-learning LET estimators and new 2026 work on LET prediction with uncertainty, but the major opportunity is to connect fast physics with dose, LET, uncertainty, and ultimately outcome-linked constraints in one validated environment. citeturn32view3turn23search4turn23search0

The fourth bottleneck is **benchmarking discipline**. If I were defining success criteria for a “new best Bragg Peak simulation model,” they would not be “99% accurate versus OpenGATE” in isolation. They would be: demonstrably tight agreement to a locked high-statistics MC reference across multiple anatomies and beam angles; agreement to water-phantom and patient-specific QA measurements; explicit uncertainty estimates that correlate with failure cases; robustness across institutions, scanners, and acquisition protocols; and, ideally, improvements in LET- or outcome-relevant metrics rather than only voxel dose similarity. That synthesis follows directly from the recent proton-AI reviews, current fast-MC validation culture, and the shift toward public benchmarks like DoseRAD2026. citeturn28view3turn31view0turn7academia23turn32view3

My overall recommendation is therefore straightforward. A transformer can be useful in applied proton therapy, but the most defensible frontier use is **not** “train a transformer to imitate OpenGATE and call it done.” It is “use modern deep learning where it reduces the true bottlenecks—beam-angle handling, adaptive replanning speed, CT/MR/CBCT material inference, MC denoising, LET prediction, uncertainty estimation, or back-end acceleration—while keeping a physics engine and measurement culture at the center.” That is the path most likely to matter scientifically, medically, and eventually clinically. citeturn17view0turn22search0turn30search14turn33search0turn32view0turn32view3
